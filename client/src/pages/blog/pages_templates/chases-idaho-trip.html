Welcome to Idaho

So, you're visiting Idaho from Connecticut, huh, and wondering about the vibe? Well, let me put it this way for your nephew: Imagine back in Connecticut, it's like that very neat, very organized playroom. All the toys have to be in the exact same spot, and if you want to borrow the really cool ones, you probably need to fill out form 37B in triplicate and maybe get it notarized by the committee that oversees 'Proper Toy Usage.'

Then you land in Idaho, and it's like stepping into this giant, awesome backyard. You can build a fort out of pretty much anything, shout as loud as you want (the local government here mostly trusts you not to summon anything too weird), and the only 'traffic regulation' you might encounter is a herd of cows using their collective bargaining power to control the road. Here, if you want to, say, wear a potato sack as a fashion statement, folks might just ask where you got such a fine-looking, locally-sourced sack. Try that in some parts of Connecticut, and they might convene a special town council meeting to debate the socio-economic impact of sack-based attire. In Idaho, the general political philosophy feels a bit more like 'live and let live, as long as you're not poking the bears or trying to regulate the sunshine.' Your biggest worry might be if a moose disapproves of your hat, not whether you've filed the appropriate 'Intent to Have a Good Time' paperwork for your Tuesday.

Some Quick Facts

Idaho, a vast inland state in the Pacific Northwest, covers over 83,000 square miles, characterized by the rugged Rocky Mountains, including its highest point, Borah Peak, soaring to 12,662 feet. It features extensive wilderness, deep river canyons carved by major waterways like the Snake and Salmon Rivers, and expansive high desert plains, with its lowest point sitting at 710 feet in Lewiston. Connecticut, on the other hand, is a much smaller New England coastal state, encompassing just over 5,500 square miles. Its landscape is marked by rolling hills, forests, and a significant coastline along the Long Island Sound, which is its lowest point at sea level. Connecticut's highest point, on the slope of Mount Frissell, reaches a more modest 2,380 feet, reflecting a gentler, more compact terrain shaped by glacial activity and its proximity to the Atlantic.

The climates of the two states also differ significantly, though both generally experience four distinct seasons. Idaho's climate is incredibly varied due to its size and diverse topography, ranging from semi-arid in many of its southern plains to alpine conditions in the high mountains. Summers are typically warm to hot and dry, while winters are cold and snowy, especially at higher elevations. Connecticut experiences a humid continental climate. This means its summers are warm to hot and notably more humid than Idaho's, and its winters are cold with consistent snowfall, particularly inland. Connecticut's weather is also influenced by its coastal location, which can moderate temperatures slightly but also bring coastal storms.

Day 3

Its Friday and I am sure your in for some relaxing. First we will take a trip down to the Utah Crater.

The Utah Crater, located at the Homestead Resort in Midway, Utah, is a unique geothermal spring hidden within a 55-foot tall, beehive-shaped limestone rock. This natural wonder was formed over thousands of years and features a hot spring pool that stays consistently warm, around 90-96 degrees Fahrenheit, year-round. The crater is a popular spot for scuba diving (it's the only warm-water scuba diving destination in the continental US), snorkeling, swimming, or just enjoying a therapeutic soak. There's an opening at the top of the dome that lets in sunlight and fresh air, and visitors access the water through a tunnel. It's a pretty remarkable natural geological feature.

The Utah Crater at Homestead Resort generally welcomes visitors for a swim or soak daily, often opening around 10:00 AM or 11:00 AM and closing in the evening, typically between 6:00 PM and 8:00 PM, with slightly different schedules for weekdays versus weekends or holidays. For a swim/soak session, which usually lasts about 40 minutes, you can expect to pay around $15 per person (ages 8+) on weekdays (Monday-Thursday), and around $18 per person on weekends (Friday-Sunday) and during holiday periods.

Water too hot well before we head home we will head to bear lake (north shore in particular).

Bear Lake, which straddles the border of Utah and Idaho, is a massive natural freshwater lake famous for its stunning and intense turquoise-blue water, earning it the nickname the "Caribbean of the Rockies." This unique color is caused by microscopic calcium carbonate particles suspended in the water that reflect the blue light of the sky.

Visitors flock to its sandy beaches for swimming and sunbathing. The large size of the lake makes it perfect for all kinds of watersports like boating, jet skiing, sailing, and paddleboarding. The area is also famous for its delicious raspberries, and trying a fresh raspberry shake from one of the local shops in Garden City is a must-do tradition.

Day 4

Today we will start our day with EBR-1, the Experimental Breeder Reactor No. 1. It's a really special place because it was the world's first nuclear power plant to generate a usable amount of electricity. Imagine this: on December 20, 1951, scientists here lit up four 200-watt lightbulbs using nuclear power for the very first time! The next day, they generated enough electricity to power the entire building.

This historic site, which is now a museum and a National Historic Landmark, is located in the high desert of Idaho. Inside, you can see the control room where scientists and engineers worked, and even some of the original equipment they used. You'll get to see the wall where the team that first made electricity with nuclear energy signed their names. They also have two prototype aircraft nuclear propulsion engines in the parking lot, which were part of a project to create a nuclear-powered jet! Cool note this is where your uncle works. 

Summer hours: The EBR-1 Atomic Museum is open seven days a week from 9 a.m. to 5 p.m., Memorial Day weekend through Labor Day weekend, and admission is free.

After our incredible tour of EBR-1, where we literally saw where the atomic age of electricity began, we're going to head into the town of Arco for a well-deserved meal at Pickles Place. It's a classic local spot, so get ready for some good old-fashioned burgers and fries.

As we roll into Arco, I want you to look out for something you definitely won't expect to see in the middle of the desert: the sail of a submarine! It's from the USS Hawkbill, and it's a memorial to the submariners of the Cold War. It's a pretty amazing sight.

And here's the coolest part that connects our whole day: Arco was the very first city in the world to be entirely powered by nuclear energy. On a day in 1955, the power from a nearby experimental reactor, a successor to EBR-1, was used to light up the whole town. So, as we're eating, we'll be in the first place on Earth to have experienced what we just learned about.

Get ready for our next stop, because we're blasting off to the moon! Well, almost! We're heading to a place called Craters of the Moon, and it looks so much like another planet that the Apollo astronauts actually trained here before they went to the real moon!

First things first, when we get there, we're going to the visitor center to get you sworn in as a Junior Ranger. You'll get a cool activity book, and as we explore, you can complete the activities. Once you're done, you'll get your very own official Junior Ranger badge to show everyone what an awesome explorer you are.

Then, we'll drive along the Loop Road, and it's like a road trip on another world. We'll get out and do some amazing things:

  • Climb a Volcano: We're going to hike up Inferno Cone! It's like a giant black sandcastle, and from the top, you'll be able to see for miles and miles. We can pretend we're the kings of our very own planet.
  • Explore Secret Caves: This is the really cool part. We're going to go down into real lava tubes! These are caves that were formed by rivers of lava a long, long time ago. We'll get our permits and explore the Indian Tunnel, where we'll be walking in the footsteps of ancient explorers.
  • See a Devil's Orchard: We'll take a walk through a spooky-cool place called Devils Orchard, where the trees are all twisted and growing out of the black rock.
  • Peer into a Mini-Volcano: We'll check out the Spatter Cones, which are like baby volcanoes. We can walk right up to the top and look inside!

Day 5

Our adventure begins at the Idaho Falls Zoo, which is nestled inside the huge and beautiful Tautphaus Park. This isn't just any zoo; it's a journey across the globe! We'll start our expedition in the "Kingdom of Africa" to see the majestic lions, the kings of the savanna. Then, we'll trek to the high mountains of Asia to hopefully catch a glimpse of the rare and beautiful snow leopards. After that, we can waddle over to the penguin exhibit to see them swim and play. We'll finish our visit at the Children's Zoo, a special area where you can get up close with friendly farm animals like goats and sheep.

To be honest is a fairly small Zoo

After a quick pit stop for lunch, we'll escape the sun and dive into history at the Museum of Idaho. It’s the largest museum in the state and packed with amazing things. Their main exhibit, "Way Out West," is like a massive time machine. We'll go back 13,000 years to see the bones of giant mammoths that once roamed this very area and learn about the history and culture of the Shoshone-Bannock Tribes. Then, we'll walk right into a recreated 19th-century frontier town where we can imagine what life was like for pioneers. Before we leave, you'll get to take charge in the Marie Putnam Discovery Room, a fantastic hands-on area where you can explore a real tipi, play in a settler's cabin, and do your own science experiments.

Our grand finale is a trip under the ocean at the Idaho Aquarium. Prepare to be amazed as you walk through the incredible shark tunnel, with these powerful predators swimming right over your head. We'll get to see mysterious moray eels peeking out from their caves and spot all kinds of colorful tropical fish. The best part will be the interactive exhibits; you can actually reach into the touch tank and feel the smooth skin of a stingray as it glides by your hand! Beyond the fish, they also have a whole section for reptiles and amphibians, so we can see some cool land creatures too.

We'll take a short drive north of Idaho Falls to a place you have to see to believe: Yellowstone Bear World. This isn't a normal zoo. We'll stay safely in the car and take a drive-thru safari through a massive park where grizzly bears and black bears will be roaming free right outside our windows! We'll also see herds of bison, elk, and deer. After our drive, we can get out and explore the second part of the park, which has a petting zoo and even amusement rides.
